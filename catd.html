COMMON CAT DISEASES


CANCER

Causes of Cancer

Cancer is a “multifactorial” disease, which means it has no known single cause. However, we do know that both hereditary and environmental factors can lead to the development of cancer in cats.

Squamous cell carcinoma of the ear, eyelid or nose is a skin cancer caused by repeated exposure to the sun. White, or light colored, cats are more susceptible to squamous cell carcinoma.
Lymphosarcoma or lymphoma (LSA), is one of the most common type of cancer in cats. Some reports estimate that 30% of all reported cat cancers are due to LSA. Feline leukemia virus (FeLV) is linked to most forms of LSA except for the gastrointestinal (GI) form. FeLV is a transmittable retrovirus that can be passed in utero as well as through saliva and direct contact. Primarily a disease in younger cats, the virus doesn’t always manifest symptoms, so it is important to have your cat tested regularly to prevent transmission and progression. There is a vaccine available for FeLV that your veterinarian can discuss with you based on your cat’s lifestyle and risk of exposure to FeLV. The GI form of LSA (the most common form) can cause a large mass in the stomach or intestine or diffuse infiltration throughout the intestinal tract.

Cancer Symptoms

Symptoms of cancer in cats may include:
  • Lumps (which are not always malignant, but are always worth having a veterinarian examine)
  • Swelling
  • Persistent sores or skin infections
  • Bad breath
  • Listlessness, lethargy or other marked change in behavior
  • Decreased or loss of appetite
  • Change in behavior

Diagnosing Cancer in Cats

If a lump is present, the first step is typically a needle biopsy, which removes a very small tissue sample for microscopic examination of cells. Alternately, surgery may be performed to remove all or part of the lump for diagnosis by a pathologist. Radiographs, ultrasound, blood evaluation and other diagnostic tests may also be helpful in determining if cancer is present or if it has spread. Cats More Prone to Cancer
Though cancer can be diagnosed in cats of all ages and breeds, it is much more common in older cats. Certain breeds are prone to specific cancers, but cats with white ears and heads are particularly susceptible to skin cancer. Ask your vet if your cat falls into specific at-risk categories.

Cancer Prevention

Keeping your cat indoors will protect her from certain skin cancers caused by repeated sun exposure and sunburn. Breast cancer is a common cancer for cats, but it can be avoided by having your cat spayed before her first heat cycle.

Cancer Treatments

Treatment options vary and depend on the type and stage of cancer. Common treatments include surgery, chemotherapy, radiation and immunotherapy or a combination of therapies. Success of treatment depends on the form and extent of the cancer and the aggressiveness of the therapy. Of course, early detection is best. Some cat owners opt for no treatment of the cancer, in which case palliative care, including pain relief, should be considered. Regardless of how you proceed after a diagnosis of cancer in your pet, it is very important to consider his quality of life when making future decisions. Some cancers can be cured, and almost all patients can receive at least some benefit from treatment. Please note that if your cat’s cancer is not curable, there are still many things you can do to make your pet feel better. Don’t hesitate to talk to your vet about your options. And remember good nutrition and loving care can greatly enhance your cat’s quality of life.

Feline Diabetes

Feline diabetes, also known as diabetes mellitus in cats, is a common metabolic disorder that affects a cat's blood sugar levels. It is similar to diabetes in humans.

Symptoms of Feline Diabetes

  • Increased thirst (polydipsia)
  • Excessive urination (polyuria)
  • Increased appetite (polyphagia)
  • Weight loss
  • Lethargy
  • Dehydration
  • Poor coat condition
  • Vomiting
  • Weakness in the hind legs (neuropathy)

Causes of Feline Diabetes

The exact causes of feline diabetes are not well understood, but several factors can contribute to its development:

  • Obesity: Overweight cats are at higher risk.
  • Genetics: Some breeds are more predisposed to diabetes.
  • Pancreatitis: Inflammation of the pancreas can affect insulin production.
  • Stress: Chronic stress can impact a cat's hormonal balance.

Treating Feline Diabetes

Feline diabetes can be managed, and in some cases, remission is possible. Treatment typically involves:

  • Insulin therapy: Regular insulin injections are often required.
  • Diet: Special diabetic cat food can help regulate blood sugar.
  • Weight management: Achieving and maintaining a healthy weight is crucial.
  • Monitoring: Regular blood glucose monitoring is essential.

Prevention of Feline Diabetes

While not all cases of feline diabetes can be prevented, you can reduce the risk by:

  • Feeding a balanced diet and avoiding excessive treats.
  • Promoting exercise and play to maintain a healthy weight.
  • Regular veterinary check-ups for early detection.
  • Reducing stress in your cat's environment.

It's important to consult with a veterinarian for a proper diagnosis and personalized treatment plan if you suspect your cat has diabetes.

High-Rise Syndrome in Cats

High-Rise Syndrome is a term used to describe injuries that cats may sustain when falling from a significant height, often from high-rise buildings or apartment complexes.

Symptoms of High-Rise Syndrome

  • Fractured bones
  • Head trauma
  • Internal injuries
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Lacerations

Causes of High-Rise Syndrome

The causes of High-Rise Syndrome in cats often include:

  • Curiosity: Cats may climb onto balconies or windowsills out of curiosity.
  • Predatory instincts: They may be chasing after birds or insects and lose balance.
  • Poorly secured windows or balconies: Cats can slip through gaps or jump over low barriers.

Treating High-Rise Syndrome

Treatment for High-Rise Syndrome injuries requires immediate veterinary care:

  • Emergency evaluation to assess the extent of injuries.
  • X-rays and imaging to identify fractures or internal injuries.
  • Surgery for broken bones or severe injuries.
  • Intensive care and monitoring.

Prevention of High-Rise Syndrome

Preventing High-Rise Syndrome is crucial to protect your cat:

  • Ensure all windows and balconies are secure with appropriate barriers or screens.
  • Supervise your cat near open windows or balconies.
  • Provide indoor entertainment and stimulation to reduce their interest in high places.

Ringworm in Cats

Ringworm is a common fungal infection in cats that can affect their skin, fur, and sometimes even humans. Despite its name, it's not caused by a worm but by various fungi.

Symptoms of Ringworm

  • Round, raised, red rings on the skin
  • Patchy hair loss, often in circular patterns
  • Itchy or inflamed skin
  • Broken or brittle fur
  • Crusting or scaling of the skin

Causes of Ringworm

Ringworm is caused by several species of fungi and can be contracted through:

  • Direct contact with an infected animal
  • Indirect contact with contaminated objects or surfaces
  • Poor grooming and hygiene

Treating Ringworm in Cats

Treatment for ringworm typically involves:

  • Antifungal medications, often administered orally or topically
  • Cleaning and disinfecting the cat's environment
  • Isolating the infected cat to prevent spreading
  • Regular monitoring and follow-up with a veterinarian

Prevention of Ringworm

To prevent ringworm in cats, you can take the following measures:

  • Keep your cat's living area clean and well-maintained
  • Avoid contact with infected animals
  • Regularly groom and inspect your cat's fur and skin
  • Isolate new or sick cats until they are confirmed ringworm-free
